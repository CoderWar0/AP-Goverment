Comprehensive Study Guide

Deep-dive into each unit — all the content you need.

Enlightenment Influences

The Founders drew from Enlightenment thinkers. John Locke argued for natural rights (life, liberty, property) and government by consent — ideas that shaped the Declaration of Independence. Montesquieu advocated separation of powers into legislative, executive, and judicial branches. Rousseau developed the social contract theory: government is legitimate only when it has the consent of the governed.

Declaration of Independence (1776)

Written by Thomas Jefferson, the Declaration articulated the philosophical justification for revolution. It asserts that people have unalienable natural rights, government derives its power from the consent of the governed, and people have the right to alter or abolish a government that fails to protect their rights. It is one of the 9 required foundational documents.

Articles of Confederation (1781–1789)

The first governing document of the U.S. created a weak central government with no executive branch, no national judiciary, no power to tax, no power to regulate commerce, and required unanimous consent to amend. Shays' Rebellion (1786–87) exposed its weaknesses and spurred the call for a Constitutional Convention.

Constitutional Convention & Key Compromises

  • Great (Connecticut) Compromise: Bicameral legislature — Senate with equal representation + House with proportional representation.
  • Three-Fifths Compromise: Enslaved persons counted as 3/5 of a person for representation and taxation.
  • Electoral College: Indirect election of the president — compromise between congressional selection and direct popular vote.
  • Commerce & Slave Trade Compromise: Congress could regulate interstate commerce but couldn't ban the slave trade for 20 years.

Constitutional Principles

  • Separation of Powers: Three branches with distinct functions.
  • Checks & Balances: Each branch can limit the others.
  • Federalism: Power divided between national and state governments.
  • Republicanism: Representative democracy — elected officials govern on behalf of the people.
  • Popular Sovereignty: Government authority comes from the people.
  • Limited Government: Government power is restricted by the Constitution.

Federalist vs. Anti-Federalist Debate

  • Federalists (Hamilton, Madison, Jay): Supported ratification. A large republic controls factions (Federalist No. 10). Separation of powers prevents tyranny (Federalist No. 51). Strong executive needed (Federalist No. 70). Independent judiciary protects rights (Federalist No. 78).
  • Anti-Federalists (Brutus No. 1): Feared a powerful central government. Believed a large republic couldn't represent the people effectively. Demanded a Bill of Rights as a condition for ratification.

Federalism in Practice

  • Enumerated Powers: Explicitly granted to Congress in Article I, Sec. 8.
  • Necessary & Proper (Elastic) Clause: Grants implied powers to Congress.
  • Commerce Clause: Power to regulate interstate commerce — expanded federal authority over time.
  • Supremacy Clause: Federal law is the supreme law of the land (Article VI).
  • 10th Amendment: Powers not delegated to the federal government are reserved to the states.
  • Fiscal Federalism: Categorical grants (specific purpose), block grants (broad purpose), revenue sharing, mandates (funded and unfunded).

Congress: Structure & Powers

  • House of Representatives: 435 members, 2-year terms, proportional representation. Initiates revenue bills, impeaches officials, chooses president if no Electoral College majority. Led by Speaker of the House.
  • Senate: 100 members, 6-year terms (staggered), equal representation. Confirms presidential appointments, ratifies treaties (2/3 vote), conducts impeachment trials. Filibuster and cloture (60 votes).
  • Legislative Process: Bill introduced → committee referral → markup → rules committee (House) → floor debate → vote → conference committee (if needed) → presidential action.
  • Powers of Congress: Power of the purse, oversight, investigations, impeachment, advice and consent, declare war.

The Presidency

  • Formal (Constitutional) Powers: Commander-in-chief, veto power, appointment power, treaty-making (with Senate), pardon power, State of the Union address.
  • Informal Powers: Executive orders, executive agreements, signing statements, bully pulpit, agenda setting, party leader.
  • The Bureaucracy: 15 cabinet departments, independent agencies (NASA, CIA), independent regulatory commissions (FCC, SEC), government corporations (USPS, Amtrak).
  • Policy-Making: Iron triangles (committee + agency + interest group) and issue networks (broader coalitions of actors).

The Federal Judiciary

  • Structure: District courts → Circuit Courts of Appeals → Supreme Court (9 justices, life tenure).
  • Judicial Review: Established in Marbury v. Madison (1803) — power to declare laws/actions unconstitutional.
  • Judicial Philosophy: Judicial activism (broadly interpret the Constitution to advance rights) vs. judicial restraint (defer to the elected branches and precedent).
  • Case Selection: Writ of certiorari, Rule of Four, amicus curiae briefs.
  • Appointment Process: Presidential nomination → ABA evaluation → Senate Judiciary Committee hearings → Senate confirmation vote (simple majority).

Checks & Balances Summary

  • Legislative checks on Executive: Override veto (2/3), power of the purse, confirm appointments, ratify treaties, impeachment, War Powers Resolution.
  • Legislative checks on Judicial: Confirm judges, impeach judges, amend Constitution, change court jurisdiction.
  • Executive checks on Legislative: Veto, pocket veto, executive orders, signing statements.
  • Executive checks on Judicial: Nominate judges, pardon power.
  • Judicial checks on Legislative: Judicial review of laws.
  • Judicial checks on Executive: Judicial review of executive actions.

Bill of Rights & Selective Incorporation

The first 10 amendments protect individual liberties from the federal government. Through selective incorporation via the 14th Amendment's Due Process Clause, most Bill of Rights protections now also apply to state governments. Not all rights have been incorporated — the process has been case-by-case through Supreme Court decisions.

First Amendment Freedoms

  • Freedom of Religion: Establishment Clause (no state religion — Engel v. Vitale, school prayer banned) and Free Exercise Clause (right to practice religion — Wisconsin v. Yoder, Amish exemption from compulsory schooling).
  • Freedom of Speech: Broadly protected but not absolute. Schenck v. U.S. (clear and present danger test). Tinker v. Des Moines (student symbolic speech protected — "students don't shed their rights at the schoolhouse gate"). Time, place, and manner restrictions allowed. Unprotected speech: incitement, true threats, fighting words, obscenity.
  • Freedom of the Press: New York Times v. U.S. (prior restraint — government cannot stop publication of Pentagon Papers).
  • Assembly & Petition: Right to peacefully protest and petition the government.

Rights of the Accused

  • 4th Amendment: Unreasonable searches & seizures. Mapp v. Ohio (exclusionary rule — illegally obtained evidence inadmissible).
  • 5th Amendment: Self-incrimination, double jeopardy, due process. Miranda v. Arizona (Miranda rights — must be informed of rights during arrest).
  • 6th Amendment: Right to counsel, speedy trial, jury trial. Gideon v. Wainwright (right to attorney in felony cases, even if you can't afford one).
  • 8th Amendment: Excessive bail, cruel and unusual punishment.

Right to Privacy

Not explicitly in the Constitution but found in the "penumbras" of the 1st, 3rd, 4th, 5th, and 9th Amendments. Griswold v. Connecticut — right to privacy regarding contraception. Roe v. Wade — extended to abortion (overturned by Dobbs v. Jackson Women's Health Organization, 2022, returning the issue to states).

Civil Rights & Equal Protection

  • 14th Amendment Equal Protection Clause: No state shall deny any person equal protection of the laws.
  • Brown v. Board of Education (1954): Overturned Plessy v. Ferguson's "separate but equal" — segregated schools inherently unequal.
  • Civil Rights Act of 1964: Banned discrimination based on race, color, religion, sex, national origin in employment and public accommodations.
  • Voting Rights Act of 1965: Eliminated literacy tests and other discriminatory voting practices.
  • Levels of Judicial Scrutiny: Strict scrutiny (race, religion — compelling interest required), intermediate scrutiny (gender — important interest), rational basis (age, disability — legitimate interest).
  • Affirmative Action: Shaw v. Reno (racial gerrymandering may violate Equal Protection).

2nd Amendment

McDonald v. Chicago (2010) — incorporated the 2nd Amendment right to bear arms to apply to state and local governments through the 14th Amendment's Due Process Clause.

Political Socialization

The lifelong process of developing political attitudes and beliefs. Key agents: family (strongest early influence), schools, peers, media, religion, race/ethnicity, gender, and significant life events. Political socialization explains why people with similar backgrounds often hold similar political views.

Political Ideologies

  • Liberal: Favors government intervention in the economy (regulation, social programs, progressive taxation) but less in personal/social matters. Supports expanded civil rights, environmental protection, social safety net.
  • Conservative: Favors limited government in the economy (free market, lower taxes, deregulation) but may support government involvement in social/moral issues. Emphasizes individual responsibility, traditional values, strong defense.
  • Libertarian: Minimal government in both economic and social spheres — maximum individual freedom.
  • Populist: Government involvement in both economic and social issues — often skeptical of elites.

Public Opinion & Polling

  • Scientific Polling: Random sampling, representative sample, appropriate sample size (usually ~1,000–1,500), margin of error, careful question wording.
  • Types: Benchmark polls, tracking polls, entrance/exit polls, push polls (biased/illegitimate).
  • Challenges: Sampling error, non-response bias, question wording bias, social desirability bias, selection bias.
  • Understanding Polls: Margin of error of ±3% means the true value likely falls within 3 points of the reported number. If two candidates are within the margin of error, the race is a statistical tie.

Influences on Policy

  • Trustee Model: Representatives use their own judgment for the public good.
  • Delegate Model: Representatives vote according to constituents' wishes.
  • Politico Model: Mix of both — depends on the issue.
  • Public opinion influences agenda, but policy changes often lag behind opinion shifts.

Voting & Elections

  • Voter Turnout Factors: Education (strongest predictor), age, income, race/ethnicity, registration laws, election type (presidential vs. midterm), competitiveness of race.
  • Historical Barriers: Poll taxes (banned by 24th Amendment), literacy tests, grandfather clauses, white primaries — eliminated by 15th, 19th, 24th, 26th Amendments and the Voting Rights Act of 1965.
  • Modern Issues: Voter ID laws, gerrymandering, felon disenfranchisement, Motor Voter Act (1993), same-day registration.
  • Types of Elections: Primary (open vs. closed), general, midterm. Electoral College for presidential elections (270 to win).

Political Parties

  • Two-Party System: Democrats and Republicans dominate due to single-member district plurality (winner-take-all) voting — Duverger's Law.
  • Party Functions: Recruit candidates, mobilize voters, organize government, provide accountability, simplify voting choices.
  • Party Realignment: Major shifts in party coalitions (e.g., Southern realignment of 1960s–70s).
  • Third Parties: Rarely win but influence agendas, introduce new ideas, and can have a spoiler effect.
  • Party Decline/Dealignment: Rise of independents, split-ticket voting, weakened party identification.

Interest Groups & Lobbying

  • Types: Economic (Chamber of Commerce, AFL-CIO), ideological (ACLU, NRA), single-issue (MADD, Right to Life), public interest (Sierra Club, Common Cause).
  • Tactics: Direct lobbying, litigation (amicus curiae briefs), grassroots mobilization, electioneering, PAC/Super PAC funding, revolving door.
  • Iron Triangles: Congressional committee + bureaucratic agency + interest group = mutually beneficial policy relationship.
  • Issue Networks: Broader, more fluid coalitions of actors who influence policy.
  • Free-Rider Problem: People benefit from a group's efforts without contributing.

Campaign Finance

  • FEC: Federal Election Commission — regulates and enforces campaign finance laws.
  • Buckley v. Valeo (1976): Spending money is protected speech. Upheld contribution limits but struck down expenditure limits.
  • Citizens United v. FEC (2010): Corporations and unions can make unlimited independent expenditures. Led to rise of Super PACs.
  • PACs: Donate directly to candidates with contribution limits ($5,000/election).
  • Super PACs: Unlimited independent expenditures, cannot coordinate with campaigns, must disclose donors.
  • 501(c)(4) "Dark Money": Social welfare organizations that can engage in political activity without disclosing donors.

The Media

  • Functions: Agenda setting (what to think about), framing (how to think about it), priming (criteria for evaluation), gatekeeping, watchdog role.
  • Traditional vs. New Media: Broadcast networks, cable news, newspapers vs. social media, blogs, podcasts, online outlets.
  • Effects: Confirmation bias, echo chambers, horse-race journalism, sensationalism, 24-hour news cycle, declining trust in media.

FRQ #1 — Concept Application

Practice Prompt

A state legislature recently passed a law requiring all public school students to begin each day with a moment of silent meditation. Supporters argue this promotes discipline and focus. Opponents claim it violates constitutional protections. A group of parents has filed suit in federal court challenging the law.

(a) Identify the clause in the First Amendment that is most relevant to the parents' legal challenge.

(b) Explain how the Supreme Court's ruling in Engel v. Vitale (1962) would be relevant to the parents' case.

(c) Describe an action the parents could take, other than filing suit, to influence education policy regarding religion in schools.

(a) The Establishment Clause of the First Amendment is most relevant. It prohibits the government from making any law "respecting an establishment of religion." A state-mandated moment of silent meditation could be viewed as promoting religious activity in public schools.

(b) In Engel v. Vitale, the Supreme Court ruled that state-sponsored prayer in public schools violates the Establishment Clause, even if participation is voluntary. This precedent would support the parents' argument because a required moment of "silent meditation" could be interpreted as a government-endorsed religious practice, similar to the state-composed prayer struck down in Engel. The key principle is that the government cannot compose or mandate religious activities in public schools.

(c) The parents could lobby their state legislators directly — contacting representatives, testifying at committee hearings, or organizing petitions to repeal the law. They could also form or join an interest group focused on separation of church and state, which could engage in grassroots mobilization, run media campaigns, or support political candidates who share their position.

FRQ #1 — Concept Application

Practice Prompt 2

The federal government wants to require all states to adopt uniform environmental standards for carbon emissions. Some states argue this is an overreach of federal power. The legislation cites the Commerce Clause as its constitutional authority. Several states have threatened to challenge the law.

(a) Identify the constitutional clause the federal government is using to justify this legislation.

(b) Explain how the ruling in United States v. Lopez (1995) could be used by states to challenge this legislation.

(c) Explain how the ruling in McCulloch v. Maryland (1819) could be used by the federal government to defend this legislation.

(a) The Commerce Clause (Article I, Section 8, Clause 3), which gives Congress the power to regulate interstate commerce.

(b) In United States v. Lopez, the Court ruled that Congress exceeded its Commerce Clause authority by passing the Gun-Free School Zones Act because carrying a gun near a school did not substantially affect interstate commerce. States could argue, similarly, that local carbon emissions do not have a sufficient nexus to interstate commerce, and that the federal government is overreaching by mandating uniform state environmental standards.

(c) In McCulloch v. Maryland, the Court upheld broad congressional authority under the Necessary and Proper Clause, ruling that Congress has implied powers beyond those explicitly enumerated. The federal government could argue that regulating carbon emissions is "necessary and proper" for executing its enumerated power to regulate interstate commerce, since pollution crosses state lines and substantially affects the national economy.

(a) Describe a trend in voter turnout shown in the data.

(b) Draw a conclusion about the difference between presidential and midterm election turnout. Explain why this difference occurs.

(c) Explain how one specific reform could address the trend of lower midterm election turnout.

(a) Presidential election turnout has generally increased from 58.6% in 2012 to 66.8% in 2020. Midterm turnout spiked in 2018 (50.3%) compared to 2014 (36.7%) before declining slightly to 46.8% in 2022.

(b) Presidential elections consistently have higher voter turnout than midterm elections. For example, the 2020 presidential election had 66.8% turnout compared to 46.8% in the 2022 midterm — a gap of 20 percentage points. This occurs because presidential elections receive significantly more media coverage, generate greater public interest and engagement, and voters perceive the stakes as higher. Candidates spend more on mobilization efforts during presidential years, and presidential elections have a "coattail effect" that brings more voters to the polls.

(c) Automatic voter registration could increase midterm turnout by removing the registration barrier. Many eligible voters do not vote in midterms because they are not registered or their registration has lapsed. By automatically registering citizens when they interact with government agencies (like the DMV), states lower the cost of participation, making it more likely that people will vote even in lower-profile midterm elections.

FRQ #3 — SCOTUS Comparison

Practice Prompt

Cohen v. California (1971)

Paul Robert Cohen was arrested for wearing a jacket bearing a profane message opposing the Vietnam War draft in a Los Angeles courthouse. He was convicted of disturbing the peace under a California statute. Cohen appealed, arguing the statute violated his First Amendment right to free speech. The Supreme Court reversed the conviction, ruling that the government cannot ban speech simply because it is offensive. The Court held that Cohen's expression was protected because it was not directed at any specific person and did not constitute "fighting words."

(a) Identify the constitutional clause common to both Cohen v. California and Schenck v. United States.

(b) Based on the constitutional clause identified in part (a), explain why the facts of Schenck v. United States led to a different holding than the facts in Cohen v. California.

(c) Describe an action that Congress could take if it disagreed with the holding in Cohen v. California.

(a) The First Amendment's Free Speech Clause is common to both cases.

(b) In Schenck v. United States, the defendant distributed leaflets encouraging men to resist the draft during World War I. The Court upheld his conviction because his speech presented a "clear and present danger" — it was designed to actively undermine the military draft during wartime and could directly result in illegal action (draft evasion). In contrast, Cohen's profane message on a jacket, while offensive, was passive political expression in a public space. It was not directed at any individual, did not incite imminent lawless action, and did not constitute "fighting words." The different contexts — wartime incitement vs. passive political protest — explain why the Court restricted speech in Schenck but protected it in Cohen.

(c) Congress could propose a constitutional amendment that would narrow First Amendment protections to exclude certain types of offensive expression in government buildings. This would require a 2/3 vote in both the House and Senate and ratification by 3/4 of state legislatures. This is the only way to effectively override a Supreme Court ruling on a constitutional issue, since the Court has the final word on constitutional interpretation.

FRQ #3 — SCOTUS Comparison

Practice Prompt 2

Katzenbach v. McClung (1964)

Ollie's Barbecue was a family-owned restaurant in Birmingham, Alabama, that refused to serve Black customers inside the restaurant. The federal government argued that the restaurant's discriminatory practices could be regulated under the Commerce Clause because a substantial portion of the food it served was purchased from out-of-state suppliers. The Supreme Court agreed, ruling that Congress could use the Commerce Clause to prohibit racial discrimination in businesses that had a connection to interstate commerce, even if the connection was indirect.

(a) Identify the constitutional clause common to both Katzenbach v. McClung and United States v. Lopez.

(b) Based on the constitutional clause identified in part (a), explain why the facts of United States v. Lopez led to a different holding than Katzenbach v. McClung.

(c) Describe an action that Congress could take to address the issue raised in United States v. Lopez without relying on the Commerce Clause.

(a) The Commerce Clause (Article I, Section 8) is common to both cases.

(b) In Katzenbach v. McClung, the Court found a sufficient connection to interstate commerce because Ollie's Barbecue purchased food from out-of-state suppliers. Discrimination in restaurants that use interstate goods substantially affects interstate commerce by discouraging travel and reducing the flow of goods. In United States v. Lopez, however, the Court found that carrying a gun in a school zone did not have a substantial effect on interstate commerce. The activity was too removed from economic activity for the Commerce Clause to apply. The key difference is the degree of connection to economic/commercial activity: purchasing food from interstate suppliers has a clear commercial nexus, while possessing a firearm near a school does not.

(c) Congress could use its spending power (the "power of the purse") by conditioning federal education funding on states passing their own gun-free school zone laws. This approach — used successfully with the drinking age (South Dakota v. Dole) — doesn't rely on the Commerce Clause but achieves the same policy goal through fiscal federalism.

FRQ #4 — Argument Essay

Practice Prompt

Develop an argument about whether the Electoral College should be maintained or replaced by a national popular vote for electing the president.

In your essay:

  • Articulate a defensible claim or thesis
  • Support your claim with at least TWO pieces of specific and relevant evidence (from foundational documents, SCOTUS cases, the Constitution, or course concepts)
  • Use reasoning to explain why your evidence supports your claim
  • Respond to an opposing or alternate perspective

Sample Thesis (Pro–Electoral College): The Electoral College should be maintained because it upholds the principles of federalism embedded in the Constitution and ensures that less populated states retain a meaningful voice in presidential elections.

Evidence 1: The Great Compromise at the Constitutional Convention balanced large-state and small-state interests by creating a bicameral legislature. The Electoral College extends this same logic to presidential elections — giving each state electors based on its total congressional representation (House + Senate). This means small states have a slightly larger per-capita voice, which mirrors the Founders' intent to balance popular rule with state sovereignty.

Evidence 2: In Federalist No. 68, Alexander Hamilton argued that the Electoral College would ensure that the president is chosen by those most capable of analyzing the qualities needed for the office, and would prevent a demagogue from taking power through direct popular appeal alone. While modern electors rarely exercise independent judgment, the structural purpose of filtering direct democracy remains relevant.

Counterargument & Rebuttal: Opponents argue the Electoral College is undemocratic because a candidate can win without the popular vote (as in 2000 and 2016). While this is a valid concern, the United States is a federal republic, not a pure democracy. Federalist No. 10 warns against the "tyranny of the majority" — Madison argued that pure majority rule can trample minority rights. The Electoral College ensures that candidates must build geographically broad coalitions rather than focusing only on high-population urban centers, which serves the Founders' goal of preventing faction dominance.

FRQ #4 — Argument Essay

Practice Prompt 2

Develop an argument about whether the power of the federal government relative to state governments has grown too large, or whether this expansion has been beneficial for protecting individual rights.

In your essay:

  • Articulate a defensible claim or thesis
  • Support your claim with at least TWO pieces of specific and relevant evidence
  • Use reasoning to explain why your evidence supports your claim
  • Respond to an opposing or alternate perspective

Sample Thesis (Federal expansion has been beneficial): The expansion of federal power relative to state governments has been essential for protecting individual rights and ensuring equal treatment under the law, particularly for historically marginalized groups.

Evidence 1: Brown v. Board of Education (1954) — The Supreme Court used federal judicial power to overturn state-mandated racial segregation in public schools. Without the authority of the federal government to enforce desegregation against resistant state governments, equal protection under the 14th Amendment would have remained hollow. This demonstrates that federal power was necessary to protect civil rights when states refused to do so.

Evidence 2: The 14th Amendment and selective incorporation — Through a series of Supreme Court decisions, the federal government has applied most Bill of Rights protections to state governments. Cases like Gideon v. Wainwright (right to counsel) and Mapp v. Ohio (exclusionary rule) ensured that individuals had consistent constitutional protections regardless of which state they lived in. Without federal incorporation, states could have denied fundamental rights to their residents.

Counterargument & Rebuttal: Anti-Federalists, as articulated in Brutus No. 1, warned that a powerful central government would become tyrannical and unresponsive to local needs. While this concern has some merit — unfunded mandates and "one-size-fits-all" policies can burden states — the historical record shows that states were often the primary violators of individual rights (slavery, Jim Crow, denial of due process). Federal intervention has been essential to ensure that constitutional guarantees are not merely aspirational but are actually enforced.

Exam Day Strategies

Proven tips to help you score a 4 or 5!

Multiple-Choice Strategy

Read ALL answer choices before selecting. The AP exam often has answers that are partially correct but not the BEST answer. Eliminate obviously wrong answers first. Look for qualifiers like "always," "never," "most," "least" — extreme words are often wrong. There is no penalty for guessing, so never leave a question blank. Budget ~1.5 minutes per question.

Concept Application FRQ

You'll get a real-world scenario. Define the concept clearly. Apply it directly to the scenario using specific details from the stimulus. Explain the connection — don't just name-drop. One paragraph per part is usually sufficient.

Quantitative Analysis FRQ

Cite specific numbers from the data ("turnout increased from 36.7% to 50.3%"). Describe the trend first, then explain a course concept or political principle that accounts for the pattern. Don't just describe — analyze.

SCOTUS Comparison FRQ

Know ALL 15 required cases cold — facts, holding, and constitutional principle. The prompt describes a NON-required case and pairs it with a required one. Identify the shared constitutional principle. Then explain how different facts led to different holdings. Be specific about what distinguished the two cases.

Argument Essay

Take a clear position in your thesis. Support with 2–3 strong pieces of evidence (foundational documents, SCOTUS cases, amendments, course concepts). Each piece of evidence needs reasoning — explain WHY it supports your argument. Address a counterargument and explain why your position is still stronger. Aim for a well-organized 4–5 paragraph essay.

15 Required SCOTUS Cases

Marbury v. Madison, McCulloch v. Maryland, Schenck v. U.S., Brown v. Board, Baker v. Carr, Engel v. Vitale, Gideon v. Wainwright, Tinker v. Des Moines, NYT v. U.S., Wisconsin v. Yoder, Roe v. Wade, Shaw v. Reno, U.S. v. Lopez, McDonald v. Chicago, Citizens United v. FEC.

9 Required Foundational Documents

Declaration of Independence, Articles of Confederation, U.S. Constitution, Federalist No. 10, Federalist No. 51, Federalist No. 70, Federalist No. 78, Brutus No. 1, Letter from Birmingham Jail (MLK Jr.).

Time Management

Section I (MCQ): 80 minutes for 55 questions = ~1.5 min each. Section II (FRQ): 100 minutes for 4 questions. Suggested split: ~20 minutes for FRQs 1–3 each, ~40 minutes for the Argument Essay (FRQ 4). Always outline FRQ 4 before writing!
